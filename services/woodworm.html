Understanding the Problem

What is Woodworm?

"Woodworm" is the collective name for the larvae of several species of wood-boring beetle. The most common in UK buildings is the Common Furniture Beetle (Anobium punctatum), though the House Longhorn Beetle, Deathwatch Beetle and Powderpost Beetle are also found.

The adult beetle lays eggs in cracks and crevices in timber. When the larvae hatch, they bore through the wood for several years before pupating and emerging as adult beetles — creating the characteristic small round exit holes (1–2mm for Common Furniture Beetle) and leaving behind a fine powdery dust called frass.

The exit holes you see represent beetles that have already left. The active infestation is the larvae still inside the timber. This is why a proper survey matters: old, inactive woodworm (beetles long gone) does not need treatment. Active woodworm — with fresh frass and live larvae — does. Treating inactive woodworm is unnecessary cost; missing active woodworm allows structural damage to continue.

Woodworm is most commonly found in softwood roof timbers, ground-floor joists, floorboards, staircases and antique furniture. Damp timber is particularly attractive to egg-laying beetles, making properties with existing damp problems especially vulnerable.

Warning Signs

  • Small round holes (1–2mm) in floorboards, beams or furniture
  • Fine powdery dust (frass) below or around timber — especially if fresh
  • Weak, crumbling timber that breaks apart easily
  • Dead beetles found on windowsills near timber during spring/summer
  • Holes with clean, sharp edges and fresh cream-coloured frass (active)
Active vs Inactive?

Fresh frass is cream-coloured and lemon-shaped. Old frass is grey and compacted. We check for live larvae and fresh emergence holes to confirm whether treatment is needed.

Our Approach

How We Survey & Treat Woodworm

1

Survey & Activity Confirmation

We inspect all accessible timber thoroughly — roof space, subfloor void, staircases and visible structural timbers. We look for fresh frass, clean exit holes and evidence of live larvae to confirm whether the infestation is active before recommending any treatment.

2

Structural Assessment

We assess whether affected structural timbers — joists, rafters, purlins — have been weakened to the point where repair or reinforcement is needed, in addition to insecticide treatment.

3

Specialist Insecticide Treatment

We apply a specialist boron-based or permethrin insecticide treatment by spray, injection or paste depending on timber type and accessibility. The treatment penetrates the timber and kills larvae on contact. All products used are compliant with current UK regulations.

4

Damp Check & Advice

Because damp timber attracts wood-boring beetles, we check for any underlying moisture problems that may have encouraged the infestation and advise on prevention — keeping timber dry is the best long-term defence.
